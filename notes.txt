Prevention Quality Indicators (PQIs) are "Ambulatory Care Sensitive Conditions." These are illnesses 
where, if a person has good primary care, they should never end up in a hospital bed.

We use Composites (91, 92, 93) instead of individual indicators (like "Hypertension") for two reasons:

Statistical Stability: Individual indicators (like PQI #7) can have "noisy" data if a county has a small population. Composites aggregate these into a stronger, more reliable signal.

Systemic Grouping: They represent different "strains" on the hospital:

PQI 91 (Acute Composite): Includes Pneumonia and UTI. These are "sudden" hospitalizations that usually happen because of a breakdown in home health or social checks.

PQI 92 (Chronic Composite): Includes Heart Failure, COPD, and Hypertension. These represent the "revolving door" of patients who are not managing long-term conditions.

PQI 93 (Diabetes Composite): Specifically tracks Diabetes complications. We separate this because diabetes is a "force multiplier"—it often leads to the highest acuity and longest stays.

1. The "Mid-Point Split" Heuristic (Recommended)
This is the standard approach when you don't have individual birth dates. You assume that the population in the Age_60_69 block is distributed evenly. 
Therefore, you count half of that block as "Seniors" (65-69) and the other half as "Pre-Seniors" (60-64).The Formula for your 
Senior Population (65+):$$Total_{65+} = (0.5 \times Age\_60\_69) + Age\_70\_79 + Age\_80\_$$Pros: Easy to implement in your preprocessing script.Cons: 
It assumes a "flat" population pyramid between 60-69, which might slightly overestimate 65+ if there are more 60-year-olds than 69-year-olds.
